About Noble Slate

Hello. My name is George-Owen Damoyi-Bouridis. This page is my logbook and my corner of the internet. It is part portfolio, part notebook, and part memory for future me. If I did something, I want it written down. If I am still learning, I will say that.

The name Noble Slate is built from my own name. George goes back to the Greek geōrgos, “earth worker”. Owen is usually linked to Eugenius, “well-born” or “noble”. A slate is both a metamorphic rock with tight, straight cleavage under pressure and the kind of board you write on, wipe clean, and write on again.[22][23] Put that together and you get a person who learns by writing things down, under pressure, over and over. That is the whole point of this site.

Now

Right now, I’m in Grade 10 at Holy Trinity Catholic High School in Simcoe, Ontario, in the Extended French program.[1][2][3] I’m trying to build a clean public record of what I actually do, not just a list of things I say I’m “interested” in.

At the moment I’m working on three main tracks:

  1. A small thrust-vector-controlled model rocket.
    I’m sketching a simple TVC model that I can recover cleanly. That means: a two-servo gimbal around the motor, a short list of IMUs, and a basic control loop that I can actually tune without lying to myself.[10][11][12][13] I’m reading about thrust vectoring, control surfaces at high angles of attack, and what people have already done with small electric TVC rigs instead of pretending I invented any of this.[14][15][16] Failure notes, tuning attempts, and “why this blew up on the pad” will live here once I have real data.
  2. VitaTrace – an early-signal health device idea.
    I’m drafting an early wearable / service concept I call VitaTrace. The focus is simple: can we collect useful early signals on risk without drowning people in noise, especially for cancers that are usually found too late, like pancreatic cancer?[17][18][19][21] I’m writing:
    • a clear problem statement (who, what, why),
    • a basic service and data flow, and
    • a first-pass privacy checklist for Ontario.
    My dad died of pancreatic cancer when I was little, and a big part of this is me trying to understand what “early” could realistically mean, instead of just saying “I care about health” in a sentence and moving on.
  3. Getting less bad at tuba.
    In concert band I play tuba (euphonium before that). I keep basic logs: long tones, breath work, metronome tempos, what breaks when I’m tired, and what actually helped. The goal is to be able to hand those notes to a beginner so they can avoid some of my mistakes.

A running snapshot of what I’m doing at any given time will end up on a /now page in the style of nownownow.com, once I write it.

Background

I’m a student at Holy Trinity Catholic High School in Simcoe. I’m in Extended French and working toward bilingual-level French by graduation, which in Ontario usually means a set of French language credits plus other courses taught in French.[1][2][3][4][5]

At school, I’m involved in:

  • Student Council
  • HTTV announcements and A/V (soundboard and TriCaster)
  • Concert band (euphonium before, tuba now)
  • Health care club
  • Knitting club
  • Newspaper club

Outside of school, I’m in the Royal Canadian Air Cadets through 104 “Starfighter” Squadron in Brantford, which is part of the national cadet program run in partnership with the Canadian Armed Forces.[6][7][8][9] I like it because it forces you to step up, listen, and make decisions in real time instead of just reading about “leadership” on a slide.

The common thread in all of this is pretty simple: I like problems where biology, hardware, and systems thinking collide and you still have to explain the result to a normal person.

Site notes

This site is deliberately plain: black, white, and a few shades of grey. No tracking, no dark-pattern popups, no autoplay anything. Pages are written in Markdown and rendered to HTML. I care more about the content and the paper trail than the CSS.

The goal is that, a few years from now, someone can scroll back and see what I was actually doing and thinking, instead of a cleaned-up story I wrote after the fact.
