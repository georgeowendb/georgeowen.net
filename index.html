Introduction

Hello there! I am George-Owen Damoyi-Bouridis. Grade 10, Extended French, at Holy Trinity Catholic High School in Simcoe, Ontario. Most of my time consists of class, my projects and relating research, school extracurriculars, Air Cadets, and trying to better.[1]

The title of this site,Noble Slate, refers to my logbook. Anything I would like the internet to know about me will be shown here. This is mainly for myself in the future, being part memory as well.[2]

The name comes from my own name. George comes from a Greek root for an earth worker or farmer. Owen is usually translated as well born or noble. Slate is both a rock that splits into clean sheets and a surface you write on, erase, then write on again, similar to how people used roofing slate and writing slates before screens.[3] Noble Slate is a reminder to do the work, hold shape under pressure, and still be ready to erase a page when the data says I was wrong.

Long term, I'm aiming to work somewhere between aerospace medicine, mechatronics, and human health research in extreme environments. That overlap is where people worry about how bodies behave in flight and in reduced gravity, like the work done by the Aerospace Medical Association and space programs such as NASA human spaceflight research, and how to build systems that keep those bodies alive there.[4]

Goals

The topic of aerospace medicine, human physiology, and mechatronics sparks my interest. On one side there are people asking how bodies behave in flight and in reduced gravity, looking at bone loss, vision changes, and cardiovascular load, similar to the topics you see in spaceflight research summaries and ESA pages like human and robotic exploration.[7] On another side there are engineers building the systems that keep those people alive, like teams in mechatronics engineering programs and similar degrees.

I am not at that level yet. Right now I build small things: a thrust vector controlled model rocket, a desk sized attitude control stand, a simple stress logger, and a basic wearable biosensor idea. They are student projects. The point of these are to familiarize myself with sensors, control loops, and logs.[8]

Current Projects

Anything listed here is either actively being worked on or has a proper write up somewhere in my notes.

  • Azimuth v1: a small model rocket with a basic thrust vector control system. It uses a servo gimbal under the motor, an IMU, and a simple control loop to stop it from tipping over as soon as it leaves the rail.[9] I read rocketry basics from groups like the National Association of Rocketry and NASA material such as its rockets notes, plus control intros like the Michigan control tutorials to get ideas I can scale down safely.[10]
  • VitaTrace: a wearable biosensor idea aimed at earlier risk signals for serious disease. It is not a medical device and not a startup. It is a way to think through what a real early warning system would need if it wanted to catch something like pancreatic cancer before it is too late, and what simple sensors might actually be useful.[11] I read around topics like wearable biosensor reviews and health monitoring summaries, then cut it back to what a student can actually test.
  • Nullvector: a desk sized attitude control test stand. Instead of throwing untested code on a rocket, I bolt the hardware here, feed it IMU data, and watch what happens while nothing important is in the line of fire. It exists so that when I break control ideas, I do it on a stand that can be reset, not in a field with pieces everywhere.[12]
  • Blackwatch: a small black box for humans that logs heart rate, movement, and environment during drills or workouts. The idea is similar to flight recorders: either the data is there or it is not. I use it to see how my body reacts under load instead of guessing from how it felt afterwards, similar to how stress response articles break down what is actually going on.[13]

None of these are polished. The win is getting real data instead of only vibes.

Where my time goes

Outside of building things, most of my time is split between school, music, robotics, cadets, and exchange prep.

  • School. Grade 10 in the Extended French stream at Holy Trinity. Involved in student council, HTTV announcements, the A/V crew, concert band, robotics, healthcare club, and the school newspaper. This keeps me bouncing between writing, wiring, and trying to not miss deadlines.[14]
  • Music. I play euphonium and tuba in concert band and keep a basic practice log for long tones, breath work, and pieces. I use things like the Yamaha tuba guide and simple brass warmups so I am not guessing every time I pick up the horn.
  • Robotics. VEX robotics at school, building and debugging small competition robots with hardware from VEX Robotics. That is where I get practice wiring, bolting things together, and fixing code on a short clock.
  • Air Cadets. Cadet at 104 Starfighter Royal Canadian Air Cadet Squadron in Brantford. Parade nights, band, range, ground school, and effective speaking. It is one of the few places where adults expect you to fix problems instead of stacking excuses.[15]
  • Rotary Youth Exchange. I am applying for Rotary Youth Exchange. If it works, that means a year in another country, living with host families, going to school there, and trying to keep projects and grades moving while being the foreign student.

How this site is built

The stack is intentionally boring. I write pages in plain text using Markdown, render to HTML, and use a small amount of CSS for layout. There is one script for the search wrapper and one for the reading progress bar and section reveal.[16]

Files live in a Git repository and are served as static pages through GitHub Pages in front of Cloudflare Pages. If I ever switch tools, the rule is that I should still be able to fix the site myself on a weekend instead of waiting on a platform update.

The design stays black and white so it stays readable on school laptops, older monitors, and weak connections. If the page loads quickly, stays clear, and does not fight the content, it is doing its job.
