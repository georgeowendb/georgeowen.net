Front page

My name is George-Owen Damoyi-Bouridis. I am in Grade 10 in the Extended French stream at Holy Trinity Catholic High School in Simcoe, Ontario. This site is my logbook and my corner of the internet. It is part portfolio, part notebook, and part memory for future me.[1]

If I build something, it should show up here somewhere. If I am stuck on something, I try to write that down too. The point is not to look polished. The point is to make it possible to see what actually happened.

The name Noble Slate comes from my own name. George traces back to a Greek root for an earth worker or farmer, someone who turns soil over by hand. Owen is usually glossed as well born or noble. Slate is a rock that keeps a clean edge under pressure and it is also a surface you can write on, erase, and write on again. Put together, it is a reminder to do the work, hold shape when things are loud, and still be willing to rewrite if the data says I was wrong.[2]

What I am working on now

Most of my time goes into a few long term builds that sit between rocketry, instrumentation, and human health:

  • Azimuth v1 - a thrust vector controlled model rocket with onboard logging. I am working on servo gimbals, a short list of IMU boards, and a simple PID controller so I can get basic attitude control instead of a pure ballistic flight. Early notes will live on the Azimuth section once I publish them.
  • VitaTrace - a small wearable biosensor idea that started after my father died from pancreatic cancer. I am logging simple signals like heart rate and motion and reading about screening and early detection so I can understand what an honest early warning system would need, even at a student level. There is a more detailed outline on VitaTrace.
  • Nullvector - a desk sized attitude control test stand. I use it to tune control code with an IMU and servos on the bench first, then move that code onto actual rockets only after it behaves the way I think it should.
  • Blackwatch - a small stress logger, basically a black box for humans. It records heart rate, movement, and environment during drills or workouts so I can see how my body reacts under load instead of guessing from how it felt.[4]

These projects are not products or companies. They are experiments that let me learn control, sensors, data, and basic human physiology in a way I can actually test and measure.[5]

School and cadets

At school I try to stay useful instead of just collecting clubs. I am on student council, help run the A V setup and TriCaster for HTTV morning announcements, play euphonium and tuba in concert band, curl with the school team, build robots with the VEX club, and write for the school newspaper.[6]

Outside school I am a cadet with 104 Starfighter Royal Canadian Air Cadet Squadron in Brantford. Parade nights, band, range, ground school, and effective speaking are slow ways to get better at discipline, followership, and leadership. It is also the place where I am learning how to show up on time, in the correct order of dress, without excuses.

Long term I want to work somewhere between aerospace medicine, mechatronics, and human health in extreme environments. That interest keeps showing up in what I read, the projects I pick, and the way I think about risk and safety.[7]

Build notes for this site

Pages start as plain text files. I write in Markdown, render to HTML, and style with a small amount of CSS. There is no framework, no animations, and no client side extras outside of the search wrapper and the small side dock.

The site is currently just static files on GitHub Pages in front of Cloudflare Pages. If the stack ever changes, the rule is that it has to stay simple enough that I can fix it on a weekend without help.[9]

I try to keep the layout mostly black and white, with readable font sizes and no surprises. If the text is readable on a cheap laptop with a bad connection, that is good enough.
