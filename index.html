Front page

My name is George-Owen Damoyi-Bouridis. I am in Grade 10 in an Extended French program in southern Ontario and I spend most of my time at Holy Trinity Catholic High School in Simcoe. This site is my logbook and my corner of the internet. It is part portfolio, part notebook, and part memory for future me.[1]

If I build something, it should eventually show up here. If I mess something up, the failure notes should also show up here. I would rather have an honest trail of attempts than a fake highlight reel that skips the hard parts.

The name Noble Slate comes from my own name. The name George is linked to a Greek root for an earth worker or farmer, someone who turns soil over by hand. Owen is usually translated as well born or noble. Slate is a rock that can be split into thin, clean sheets and is also a surface people use to write on, erase, and write on again. Put together, Noble Slate is a reminder to do the work, hold shape under pressure, and still be willing to erase and rewrite if the data says I was wrong.[2]

How to read this site

The front page is a high level map. Everything detailed lives on subpages.

If you are here to see what I am doing right now, the Now page is the best place to start. If you want to see hardware builds, Projects is where I keep diagrams, logs, and crash reports. For longer notes where I try to explain one idea cleanly, Writing is the spot I use.

I try to keep each page small enough to read in one sitting and detailed enough that future me can actually reconstruct what I was thinking at the time. When I forget why a decision was made, I want to be able to follow the links and find the context.[4]

Direction and interests

Long term I want to work somewhere between medicine, engineering, and flight. That could look like aerospace medicine, human systems integration, or research on how to keep crews healthy in places that are not designed for humans.

This shows up in the things I read and build. I keep coming back to control systems, sensors, human physiology, and how to describe risk without exaggerating or minimizing it. Most of my projects are small and student level, but they aim in the same direction.

Current hardware projects

I try not to start projects unless I intend to finish them or at least write a proper postmortem. Right now there are four main builds that keep coming up.

  • Azimuth v1 – a model rocket with a basic thrust vector control system. I am working with a small servo gimbal, an inertial measurement unit and a simple control loop to keep the rocket pointed roughly where it should be instead of letting it tumble. Reading list for this project includes beginner rocketry texts and control basics from places like NASA rocketry resources and simple control theory notes from engineering departments.[5] Early diagrams and logs will land under Azimuth.
  • VitaTrace – a small wearable biosensor idea that grew out of personal experience. When my father died of pancreatic cancer, I started reading about how late it is usually caught and what is being done around early detection. VitaTrace is my attempt at a student level exploration of what an honest early warning system might need: careful sensing, clear limits, and no hype.[6] Notes live at VitaTrace.
  • Nullvector – a desk sized attitude control test stand. Instead of testing control code for Azimuth on an actual rocket first, I use this stand to run closed loop tests where nothing can crash except the code and my ego. The stand is a way to learn from small failures while everything is still on the table.
  • Blackwatch – a stress logger, basically a black box for humans during drills, workouts, or stressful events. It records heart rate, movement, and environment, then I look at how my body actually reacts compared to how it felt. It is heavily inspired by reading on cardiovascular health and by the idea of objective records for incidents.[7]

None of these projects pretend to be medical devices or production systems. They are experiments that teach me sensors, control, data, and patience.

School, cadets, and other roles

During the week I am mostly at Holy Trinity Catholic High School. I try to be useful instead of just stacking clubs on a resume. Things I am involved in right now:

  • Student council and the HTTV morning announcement crew, where I help run the soundboard and a TriCaster for live video.
  • Concert band, where I play euphonium and tuba and slowly build a better low register. I keep a practice log and read guides from places like the Yamaha tuba overview and brass pedagogy notes so I am not guessing every time.[8]
  • VEX robotics club, where we build, wire, and debug robots for competitions, using parts from VEX Robotics.
  • Curling with the school team, which is a quiet way to learn strategy, patience, and how to stay upright on ice.
  • Writing pieces for the school newspaper, mostly around science, technology, or school events.

Outside school I am a cadet with the Canadian Cadet Program, specifically 104 Starfighter Royal Canadian Air Cadet Squadron in Brantford. Parade nights, band, range, ground school, and effective speaking are slow ways to get better at discipline, followership, and leadership. It is where I am learning to show up in the right order of dress, on time, without excuses.[9]

How this site is built

The site is deliberately simple. Content starts as plain text, usually in a text editor. I write in Markdown, render to HTML, and style with a small amount of CSS. There is no framework and no heavy client side code.

Everything is currently hosted as static files on a Git repository and served through a simple static hosting setup using services like GitHub Pages and Cloudflare Pages. If I change anything about the stack, the rule is that I should still be able to fix it alone on a weekend.[10]

The design is mostly black and white on purpose. It keeps the focus on text and makes it easier to read on cheap laptops, school machines, and weak connections. If the site loads quickly and stays readable, then it is doing its job.
