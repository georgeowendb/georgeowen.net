Front page

I am George-Owen Damoyi-Bouridis. Grade 10, Extended French, at Holy Trinity Catholic High School in Simcoe. Most days I bounce between class, hardware projects, band, Air Cadets, and trying to keep up with assignments.[1]

Noble Slate is my logbook. If I build something, break something, or learn something the hard way, it should end up on this site somewhere. It is mainly for future me, but it is public so I cannot lie to myself about what I actually did versus what I planned.[2]

The name comes from my own name. George comes from a Greek root for an earth worker or farmer. Owen is usually glossed as well born or noble. Slate is a rock that splits into clean sheets and a surface you write on, erase, then write on again. Noble Slate is my reminder to do the work, hold shape under pressure, and still be ready to erase a page if the data says I was wrong.

Long term I want to work somewhere between aerospace medicine, mechatronics, and human health in extreme environments. Basically keeping people alive and stable where they are not supposed to be, using decent engineering and honest data instead of wishful thinking.[3]

What this site is for

This page is just the front door. The real detail lives under Now, Projects, and Writing.

If you want current status, go to Now. If you want schematics, code, or failure logs, go to Projects. If you want longer write ups where I try to explain one idea cleanly, go to Writing.

I try to keep each page short enough to read in one sitting. The idea is that future me should be able to rebuild what I was thinking from what is here, not guess from one sentence in a random notebook.[5]

Direction: space, health, hardware

The overlap that keeps pulling me back is aerospace medicine, human physiology, and mechatronics. On one side there are people thinking about how bodies behave in flight and in orbit, through groups like the Aerospace Medical Association and NASA's Human Research Program. On another side there are engineers building the systems that keep humans alive in those environments, similar to what programs like mechatronics engineering train people for.

I am not anywhere near that level yet. Right now I build small things: a thrust vector controlled model rocket, a desk sized attitude control stand, a simple stress logger, and a basic wearable biosensor idea. They are student projects. The point is to get comfortable with sensors, control loops, and logs, and to not hide when something fails.[6]

Current projects

Anything listed here is either actively moving or has a proper postmortem.

  • Azimuth v1 – a small model rocket with a basic thrust vector control system. I am using a servo gimbal, an inertial measurement unit, and a simple control loop to keep it from tipping over as soon as it leaves the rod. I read rocketry material from groups like the National Association of Rocketry and control basics like the Michigan Control Tutorials.[7] Early diagrams and logs live under projects.html#azimuth.
  • VitaTrace – a wearable biosensor idea aimed at earlier risk signals for serious disease. It is not a medical device and not a startup pitch. It is my way of thinking through what a real early warning system would actually need. Some of the direction is shaped by reading about pancreatic cancer and how late it is usually diagnosed.[8] Notes are under projects.html#vitatrace.
  • Nullvector – a desk sized attitude control test stand. Instead of throwing untested code on a rocket, I bolt the hardware here, feed it IMU data, and see what happens where nothing critical can burn. It exists so I can break control ideas safely and read what the logs say afterward.
  • Blackwatch – a small "black box for humans" that logs heart rate, movement, and environment during drills or workouts. It is built around the same idea as flight recorders: either the data is there or it is not.[9]

None of this is polished. The win is getting real data and not pretending something worked when it did not.

Where my time goes

Outside of building things, most of my time is in school, cadets, and a few clubs.

  • School – Grade 10 in the Extended French stream at Holy Trinity. Involved in student council, HTTV announcements, the A/V crew, concert band, robotics, healthcare club, and the school newspaper.[10]
  • Music – I play euphonium and tuba in concert band and keep a basic practice log for long tones and breath work. I read things like the Yamaha tuba guide so I am not guessing every time I pick up the horn.
  • Robotics – VEX robotics at school, building and debugging small competition robots with hardware from VEX Robotics. It is a good way to practice wiring, simple mechanisms, and code under a deadline.
  • Air Cadets – Cadet at 104 Starfighter Royal Canadian Air Cadet Squadron in Brantford. Parade nights, band, range, ground school, and effective speaking. It is one of the few places where people will tell you directly that you are out of dress or late and expect you to just fix it.[11]
  • Rotary Youth Exchange – I am applying for Rotary Youth Exchange. If it works out, it means a year in another country, living with host families, going to school there, and learning to handle being the foreign kid while keeping grades and projects moving.

How this is built

The stack is intentionally boring. I write pages in plain text using Markdown, render to HTML, and use a small amount of CSS for layout. There is a small script for the search wrapper and the reading progress bar at the top.

Files live in a Git repository and are served as static pages through GitHub Pages in front of Cloudflare Pages. If I ever switch tools, the rule is that I should still be able to fix the site by myself on a weekend.[12]

The design stays black and white so it is readable on school laptops, older monitors, and weak connections. If the page loads quickly, stays clear, and does not fight the content, it is doing its job.
