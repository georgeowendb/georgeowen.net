Hello there!

I am George-Owen Damoyi-Bouridis.

I am in Grade 10 at Holy Trinity Catholic High School in Simcoe, Ontario, in the Extended French stream [1]. Most of my days are some mix of class, reading and research, working on my own builds, concert band, Air Cadets, school clubs, and helping my mother and family where I can.

This site, Noble Slate, is my logbook and my corner of the internet. When I build something, try something, break something, or delve deep into a topic, it should end up somewhere here. It is public, but it is also for my future self. I see it as a mix of logbook, memory, and tool that my older self may use or benefit from in ways that my family's Google Photos catalogue never could [2].

Noble Slate is derived from my own name, given to honour my two grandfathers, George and Owen. George is often attributed to working the ground, or an “earth-worker.” Owen is often defined as noble or well-born. Slate is both a rock and a surface you can write on, wipe, and reuse. Put together, Noble Slate is my way of approaching this world, by doing the work that needs to be done, holding my shape when life throws punches and pressure builds, and fixing my mindset when I come across something new [3].

Since I was little, I have gazed upon the vast night sky in awe, and have wondered what happens to our fragile human bodies when we take them out of the lithosphere. That curiosity today has led me toward what I want to pursue, which is aerospace medicine, researching human physiology in foreign environments and extreme environments, and using mechatronics and sensors as a way to support and strengthen the human body and the systems around it. I am especially interested in how the cardiovascular system behaves in microgravity and adapts there, how full, healthy interstellar travel can be achieved within a single lifetime without handwaving the physics, and how biosignals can help keep crew members safe on distant missions and future interstellar ventures through real data and sensing.

Part of that interest comes from my late father, who died from pancreatic cancer when I was a child. Another part comes from being fascinated by the idea of humanity eventually stepping out of this shell we are trapped in and finding a way to survive in environments we do not fully understand yet. My goal is to work where aerospace, medicine, and mechatronics overlap, and to use my current self-directed projects to get closer to understanding these concepts in practice over time.

This front page is just the overview. If you would like to check out the more detailed and intricate work published on my site, it lives under the other pages found in the header.

To my future self, if you are reading this and it feels old or off, update it!

We were born to inherit the stars. Per aspera ad astra.
