Now

Short version: I am a Grade 10 student in the extended French stream, learning how to keep humans alive and useful in places we are not supposed to be.[3] This site is my logbook, not a polished brochure. If something happened, broke, or worked, it should land here.

My name is George-Owen Damoyi-Bouridis. I live in Port Dover, Ontario, and right now most of my time is split between school at Holy Trinity Catholic High School, Air Cadets, and a cluster of projects that sit between aerospace medicine, mechatronics, and human health in extreme environments like spaceflight and high-risk operations.[4]

Long term, I want to work on the medical side of aerospace and exploration: things like how microgravity and radiation change the body, how to monitor people in real time, and how to make sure the crew comes home in one piece. NASA’s Human Research Program and the Aerospace Medical Association are the sort of work I keep circling back to when I try to picture my future.

Right now

This is what I am actually working on, not just thinking about:

Azimuth v1 TVC model rocket

A small thrust-vector-controlled rocket that I can fully recover and learn from, instead of losing it to a tree or the lake.

I am building a custom gimbal for two micro-servos, planning a basic PID control loop around an IMU, and designing the airframe so I can actually launch this without it shredding itself. Before anything flies, I test on the bench and on a desk-sized rig called Nullvector.[5]

Notes: model rocketry safety code, recovery system that does not terrify my neighbours, and logs I will publish here even when flights fail. Relevant rabbit holes: NAR safety code, attitude control basics.

VitaTrace Wearable biosensor

A small wearable that logs simple biometrics and explores early warning signs for serious disease.

VitaTrace started as me trying to make sense of my father’s death from pancreatic cancer.[6] There is no magic sensor here. I am starting with heart rate, motion, and basic trends, then reading as much as I can about early detection and why so many people are diagnosed late.

Early influences: Pancreatic Cancer Canada, clinical trial work on better chemo regimens, and basic stats on survival and staging. The goal is not to pretend I can solve this in high school, but to learn what “better screening” would actually mean in detail.

Nullvector Attitude test stand

A desk rig where I can break control code safely before it ever rides on a motor.

Nullvector is a small frame with an IMU and servos that lets me simulate how a rocket would rotate. I am using it to tune control laws, debug sensor fusion, and get used to the feeling that what I code can physically launch itself into a wall if the math is wrong.[7]

Related reading: what an IMU actually measures, basic control systems, reaction wheels and attitude control in satellites.

Blackwatch Human stress logger

A “black box for humans” that logs heart rate, movement, and environment during drills, workouts, or rough days.

Blackwatch branches off VitaTrace. The idea is simple: treat the body like a system through training, record what happens when the load goes up, and see if I can spot patterns in how I react. That includes things like field exercises, long swim sets, or stressful school events.

This is more about honest logging than “biohacking”. It also builds the sensor and data-handling habits I will need if I end up in space health work or serious clinical environments later.

School, cadets, and Rotary

At school, I am in the Extended French program at Holy Trinity in Simcoe, with a schedule that usually includes science, math, French, and anything that lets me build or write. I help with HTTV morning announcements on soundboard and TriCaster, run A/V for liturgies and events, play tuba in concert band, sit on student council, join the healthcare club, and build robots with the VEX team.[8]

Outside of school, I am a cadet with 104 Starfighter RCACS in Brantford. That covers parade nights, band, effective speaking, ground school, and the usual mix of discipline, drills, and leadership. It is a good way to test whether I can stay useful and calm inside someone else’s structure instead of just my own projects.

I am also working through the process for Rotary Youth Exchange.[9] If that works out, this site will probably grow a whole branch about living in another country, learning a new school system from the inside, and trying to keep all of this going with a different daily routine.

This site

Noble Slate exists so I do not have to pretend that everything is a highlight reel. This is plain HTML and CSS, written by hand in a text editor, rendered as static files on GitHub Pages in front of Cloudflare. I write drafts in Markdown, then clean them before they land here.[10]

The design is intentionally simple so it does not age badly: mostly black and white, soft serif for the text, a quiet sans for navigation, no animations that need their own bug tracker, and no frameworks that would break the moment someone stops maintaining them.

If you are reading this on some ancient laptop over weak Wi-Fi and the page still loads quickly, that is by design.
